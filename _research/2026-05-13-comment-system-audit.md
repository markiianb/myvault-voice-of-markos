11. ### [**Aaron Levie**](https://www.linkedin.com/in/boxaaron?miniProfileUrn=urn%3Ali%3Afsd_profile%3AACoAAAA60_cBLDJX9LzxGGl_y-JHuiI2iWyReIo)

**Post:**

The need and opportunity for professional services and FDEs to deploy agents right now is massive.

Every tech wave offers a new era of consulting and tech services requirements. Moving from analog to digital led to a massive wave in the 90s. Moving from on-prem to cloud did the same in the 2000s. But this is going to be at a scale far greater than the others.

The reason is that agents fundamentally change the underlying workflows of an organization. Unlike most prior eras of technology, where it was a change in medium of the service being delivered (on-prem CRM to cloud CRM), agents rewire the business process itself. And unlike upgrading a tech system, business processes are full of idiosyncrasies.

Every industry will have its own variants, and every department within those industries will have variants as well. Not to mention the bespoke difference between firms. Bringing agents to marketing in CPG will look different from marketing in healthcare. Bringing agents to sales in a B2B software company will look different from a car dealership.

And none of the change is easy technically. You need to first modernize your infrastructure and data and make sure it’s ready for agents; access controls, entitlements, and permissions need to be mapped in a way that works for agents and people; you need to make sure agents have the right context to work with; you need to consistently eval and maintain the agents when there are model upgrades; and you need to drive the change management of the process itself to figure out which parts the people do and what agents do.

That’s an insane amount of technical and domain-specific process work to be done to make this all happen. Huge opportunity for new service providers, as well as internally teams and roles to emerge, to help drive this change.

## System update proposals — Voice of Markos

Drafting these comments surfaced gaps in the satellite brand system. Flagging for discussion before any chunks get edited.

### 1. Comment-craft.md is missing its own antipatterns section

The Five Failure Portraits live in `craft/antipatterns.md` but are scoped to posts. Comments need their own short antipatterns chunk. Specific comment-shaped slop I kept producing on first draft:

- **The template kudos** — "X, the Y framing/cut/read/place is right" / "X, the Y line lands." This is the LinkedIn-comment version of the Agency Wearing His Face. It signals AI more reliably than any single word does.
- **"The part doing the work" / "the part that…" construction** — recurring formula that pretends specificity. Real Markos names the thing directly.
- **The diagnostic-but-soft close** — "The interesting question is…" / "Worth watching…" / "Curious whether…" — used as a polish ending. Becomes formulaic when every comment ends this way.
- **Manufactured parallelism in 2-3 sentences** — "The floor moves, the floor isn't a moat." / "Interface was the moat, now it's the tax." Comments are short enough that one parallel construction dominates the entire comment.

Proposal: add `craft/comment-antipatterns.md` (or a section inside `comment-craft.md`) with these four patterns + before/after examples. Same casebook format as `antipatterns.md`.

### 2. The four frameworks all front-load kudos in similar shape

The kudos-first principle is correct ("there's no point pissing off the person that wrote the post"), but as written it pushes toward an open-with-praise template. Markos's own speech rarely opens with praise — he opens with the substance. Suggest reframing:

- Current: *"Pick a specific line, observation, or claim — quote or summarise it in 5–10 words. Then add value."*
- Proposed: *"Engage with the substance directly. The 'kudos' is implicit in not picking a fight — a quote or a thank-you opener is optional, not mandatory. What is mandatory: never open with disagreement, never open with a generic compliment."*

This permits short comments like "Half the pitches I've sat through this year…" (Daniel Solove) without forcing a fake "Great point" opener.

### 3. No "when not to comment" guidance beyond the Red Zone

Several posts in this batch (Gabriel Weinberg's 15-word teaser, Andrew Ng's course launch, Helen Toner's paper recommendation) don't activate any pillar substantively. Current system pushes everything toward one of the four frameworks. Need a triage rule:

- **Substantive engagement** (3-4 sentences) — when a pillar activates and a story or specific number applies
- **Brief acknowledgment** (1-2 sentences) — when the topic is adjacent but no pillar fires, or the post itself is short
- **Skip** — when the post is a link teaser, a meme, or an industry-adjacent topic with no Markos angle

Proposal: add a triage table to `comment-craft.md` mapping post type + pillar activation to comment depth.

### 4. Voice/voice.md models post lengths, not comment shapes

`voice.md § Length` says comments are 2-4 sentences but doesn't show 1-sentence "added it" vs 4-sentence Share-Experience comments. The system over-models 4-sentence engagement, which produces uniform comment shape across a day's engagement and reads as automation. Proposal: extend the length table with explicit short/medium/long comment examples.

### 5. Add a comment-specific banned-phrase list

The parent banned-words list (`leverage`, `seamless`, etc.) catches post-level slop. Comments need their own short banned-phrase list — the patterns from item 1 above plus:

- "tracks with what I've seen…"
- "the right framing/cut/read"
- "the part doing the work"
- "the interesting question is…"
- "shapes who keeps the [margin/win/edge]"
- "X is the right Y for Z"

Lowest-cost change. Could be a single subsection in `voice.md` under banned words, tagged as comment-specific.
