> Pillar 1: The builder's journey
>
> If there's one thing I learned from building a business with my father, it's to descope.
>
> He didn't start the company in a garage. It was an attic in Scotland, which I think actually suits the story better than any California origin myth.
>
> Character-based systems, dial-up, acetate slides on the projector, that was the full setup, and I used to wander up there as a kid just to see what he was working on. One day it was Windows 1.0 alpha, another day he sat me down and said (I must have been nine at this point) "that's called Unix, son, let me just show you a few things." The next thing I know, he's got me building his presentations. He'd scribble something out on paper, I'd put it together on whatever package we had at the time, he'd print it onto acetate sheets and take it to clients. I mean, I was better at it than him, which helped, and he had other things to do anyway, so we made it work.
>
> He had this instinct that when a project was sprawling, going nowhere, trying to do everything at once, the first move was always to descope. Someone would bring him something that had been failing for years and he'd look at it and say: you're trying to do too much here, a lot of this is nice-to-have and not actually needed, so let's make it workable and get it done. And they got it done.
>
> I've watched that exact same thing go wrong for my entire career, in enterprise software, in AI projects, in startups with serious funding behind them, and the frustrating part is that decades pass, the tools change completely, and the mistake stays the same. Scope creep destroys more projects than bad technology ever has, and it's almost always avoidable.
>
> Four or five years ago I sold that company (family-owned, privately held, profitable, and we never borrowed a penny to build it, which I'm proud of). It took the better part of a decade to grow it properly, and now I'm building again with MyVault.
>
> And if I'm honest, I think about those conversations in the attic more than most things I picked up in a boardroom over the years, because some things genuinely don't change, the tools do, the fundamentals don't.
>
> 🔗Follow along at myvaultai.com
>
> #Software #TechLeadership #FounderStory #TechFounder #AI

> Pillar 2: The inflection point
>
> When something goes wrong in an AI application, everyone blames the model — and most of the time, that's not where the problem is.
>
> "I'm not giving you an AI" is basically what I find myself saying when people ask about MyVault, because what we've built is a software application that happens to use AI as one of its components, one of many. But it's the other components that keep your data accurate, safe, fast, and private.
>
> Look at the architecture of any properly built AI application and you'll find a stack of components doing very specific jobs. Knowledge graphs, vector databases, embedding pipelines, security layers, API controls. All of it has to work together or the whole thing falls apart, regardless of how capable the model is.
>
> And this is why when something goes wrong, the model gets blamed. AI hallucinated, AI got it wrong. But why did it hallucinate? Usually because the retrieval layer wasn't structured properly, or the context going into the model was incomplete, or there was no verification step between output and user.
>
> When we chose Amazon Bedrock for MyVault it was about the guardrails, the privacy contracts, the legally binding agreements that your data isn't being used for training or stored. The model at that layer is secondary to the architecture around it. You can reduce temperature, give it a structured output template, and that is all it can fill in. You can verify outputs across multiple sources before anything reaches the user.
>
> People get fixated on the model question — which model are you using, which version, is it Claude, is it GPT. And I get why, it's what's being talked about everywhere. But it's a bit like asking which engine brand is in a car when what you actually want to know is whether the brakes work, whether it's safe, whether it'll get you where you're going.
>
> So when someone asks me what AI powers MyVault, it's kind of the wrong question, and I'll always tell them that.
>
> 🔗 myvaultai.com
>
> #AI #SoftwareArchitecture #DataPrivacy

### Carousel concept — design rationale

The BOTS 3 infographic answers a single insurance question through 7 layers. That's a fine static asset but it's a single example, which makes the architecture point feel domain-specific. **The carousel reframes the same 7 layers as a general pattern, with three different query types — finance, travel, insurance — to prove the breadth.** Each example is anchored to actual MyVault domain agents (`20-Product/06-Life-Domains-Framework.md`):

- Insurance Agent (F-014, in development) — flooded basement / coverage clause query
- Finance Agent (F-015, in development) — unused subscriptions query
- Travel domain (planned) — passport renewal query

These are stronger than insurance alone because they show **the same architecture solving very different questions**, which is the actual product-architecture point. They also map onto the 13 life domains marketing-narrative framework (briefing v16.3 canon).
