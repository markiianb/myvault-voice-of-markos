## The biographical arc

**Born into computing.** Markos's father took the **first computing science degree in Scotland.** That foundation set the trajectory.

**Age 9** — building graphical presentations on character-based systems, printed onto acetate sheets for overhead projectors.

**Age 12** — building company websites.

**Age 16** — first in EMEA to implement B2B marketing automation (Eloqua — now Oracle Marketing Cloud).

**Age 18** — designing a new Self-Service Web front end for Enterprise Apps.

**The family software company.** Not founded in a California garage, but **in the attic of the family home in Edinburgh.** Markos grew up inside the business, eventually **running and growing it for a decade before selling it globally.**

> "It's a lifelong thing for me. I grew up in the business."

## The inflection point — natural language computing

As a child, Markos studied programming generations with his father — from machine code to early languages. The question that stayed with him:

> "I wonder what generation language it will be when we can speak to the computer and it can program."

Now he tells **his own 12-year-old son** the same story. The son has signed up for a Claude subscription and a Lovable subscription with his pocket money.

> "The fact that you can use natural language and a little bit of structure... to get computers to automate and connect and build and communicate — it's pretty damn cool."

## The original Life App vision (30 years in waiting)

The Life App PRD was first drafted **~1995** in the unwinding of the Dot-Com era — a single, secure place to manage every dimension of a person's life. The ambition was right; the technology wasn't.

- **~1995** — First PRD sketched
- **~2023** — Scope re-ideated
- **Today** — AI (and TSH) makes the original blueprint buildable

The original scope spanned: Personal Finance, Asset Management, Investment & Retirement, Bills & Budgeting, Concierge Services, Travel & Mobility, Calendar & Tasks, Health & Wellness, Home & Automation, Family & Networking, Education & Skills, News & Philanthropy.

## The $100,000 wake-up call

While on vacation, Markos received a **$100,000 fraudulent bill from Google Cloud Platform.** The experience became a battle lasting months — dealing with automatons with no emotional intelligence, no human support, and threatened debt collectors.

This directly informed:
- MyVault's **strong preference for AWS** over GCP
- The broader philosophy of vendor accountability
- The **"trust but verify"** approach to all vendor relationships

> "When you're dealing with sensitive, classified stuff... you really go through the fine detail of security controls. That was a big eye opener."

## The core philosophy

**"Powerful, yet simple and easy to use."**

> "It should work pretty much out of the box. The heavy lifting is done in the back end."

> "It's not in one screen, it's not in one control center... it's difficult. It's a lot of moving parts. So I think simplicity — where the heavy lifting is done in the back end — that's the answer."

The frustration with current AI tools:

> "If you use Claude, use anything — it's just not easy. It's not simple. It's not in one screen."

The systemic critique: **enterprise software costs £15M of services for every £1M of license.** Consumers expect more. MyVault aims for standardized solutions that work out of the box.

## The privacy conviction

**Privacy is architecture, not a feature you bolt on after regulation.**

> "Your AI should work for you without knowing everything about you."

> "Companies waiting for rules are going to find themselves retrofitting, and that's always more expensive."

Many consumer AI apps use data for training without explicit consent. Business APIs (AWS, Azure) provide contractual guarantees.

> "The moment you give someone a tool that's genuinely private AND works well, they'll switch and tell 5 people."

## Open source — sovereign AI

> "Open source gets me absolutely fired up."

> "Why would you be paying God knows how much money to one of these companies for inference when frankly the open source has already caught up?"

The NVIDIA CEO frame, quoted by Markos:

> "I'd recommend for governments for sovereign AI. Sovereign AI, it's a little bit like personal AI, because sovereign, it's like, well, it's mine, it's got to be private, it's within my borders, it's for my country."

Markos applies it to consumers: personal AI = sovereign AI for the individual.

The pragmatic add: **"Using the right model for the right job — you shouldn't use a complex reasoning model to extract a birthday from a database."**

## The knowledge graph — the secret sauce

> "The knowledge graph is the secret sauce."

Architecture:
- **Single Source of Truth** — definitive source for documents, relationships, and metadata across the user ecosystem
- **Vector Embeddings + RDMS + Hierarchical Graph** — documents chunked, saved as embeddings, enriched with contextual summaries
- **Evaluation Flywheel** — continuous pipeline allowing new modules and newer models to plug in as they release

> "You could probably sell this thing we built to do pipeline and models evaluation — it doesn't exist off the shelf."

## Hard decisions over speed

> "The hard decisions are more effort, take more time."

> "We're going to be fanatical about speed, performance, accuracy, user experience, transparency, disclosure — and then bring the user with us on the journey."

Priorities:
- Prioritizing the knowledge graph and evaluation flywheel despite pushback on timelines
- Zero tolerance for crappy UX — "The minute you put an app in front of a consumer that's crappy, not accurate, not fast — you've lost them."
- Timing vs. trust — **"Timing is less critical than delivering a trustworthy and truly useful product. Most great products tried to launch 10-15 years ago and failed."**

## The progressive disclosure principle

> "We have the opportunity to take a step back and go — well, how would we want it?"

- Not a black box — users can dial up or dial down how much information they receive
- Transparency on what data is synced, allowing users to prioritize or revoke access anytime
- Fanatical about: speed, performance, accuracy, UX, transparency, disclosure

> "This is not just a product. It's a conviction that consumers deserve better."

## The core tagline

> "Everybody could do with their own personal digital brain with AI superpowers."

The framing question: **"What if every household had one?"**

The democratization frame:

> "We want to democratize AI. We want to make the most powerful and sophisticated capabilities available to households and individuals. Not just the mega wealthy, Fortune 1000 governments and all this stuff."

## Market opportunity (Markos's framing)

- 4.9B internet users globally who manage personal documents
- $47B knowledge management market by 2028
- 73% of consumers concerned about AI data privacy

The addressable scope: every household, every professional, every small business.
